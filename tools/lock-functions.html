Door Lockset
Functions & Cores

STANDARD: A156.2 / A156.13
CYLINDERS: A156.30
GRADES: A156.2 / A156.13
ORG: BHMA / ANSI

Lockset functions define the operational behavior of each opening — which side requires a key, which side is always free for egress, and how the latch and deadbolt interact. Functions apply across mortise (A156.13) and cylindrical (A156.2) platforms; exact codes and behavior vary by manufacturer and series. Always verify against manufacturer cut sheets before specifying.

ANSI/BHMA A156.30 · Cylinder Reference

Core & Cylinder Types

Standard Cylinder
Removal: Requires disassembly of lockset or cylinder pull tools. Not field-interchangeable without hardware.

Rekeying: By locksmith on-site or cylinder sent out. Pins replaced in place.

Most common for small-scale projects and budget work. Lower upfront cost; higher long-term cost on large keying systems.
Permanent Low Cost
SFIC — Small Format IC
Removal: Control key removes and inserts core in seconds, without tools or disassembly.

Standard: ANSI/BHMA A156.30 — cross-compatible housing across manufacturers (BEST, Corbin, Arrow, Yale, Medeco, etc.).

Industry standard for large keying systems. Core changes can be delegated to trained facilities staff. 7-pin format.
IC Standard Cross-Brand 7-Pin
LFIC — Large Format IC
Removal: Control key interchangeable, no tools required. Larger housing than SFIC.

Standard: Manufacturer-specific — Schlage FSIC, ASSA ABLOY, Sargent. Not cross-compatible with SFIC housing.

Specify manufacturer at project outset — LFIC cores are not interchangeable between brands. Confirm with hardware distributor.
Mfr-Specific Large Format
Construction Core
Purpose: Temporary cores installed at hardware rough-in, keyed alike across all openings for contractor access during construction.

Removal: Removed and replaced with owner's permanent cores at substantial completion, or upon owner's authorization.

Never include in permanent keying schedule. Collect and destroy construction keys at turnover.

Purpose: Proprietary or licensed keyway with controlled key duplication — keys cannot be copied at a standard hardware store.

Formats: Available in standard (fixed) or IC format. Examples: Medeco, Mul-T-Lock, Schlage Everest, BEST Primus.

Access Control · Electrified Openings

Electrified Hardware
& Lockset Pairing

Frame-Mounted
Electric Strike
Replaces the standard strike plate in the door frame. When a credential is accepted, the strike keeper pivots and releases — the door is pushed open without retracting the latch. The latch snaps back into the strike when the door closes.

Best paired with: Storeroom (F04) — outside lever remains permanently rigid; the strike is the only controlled entry point. Mechanical key works as backup regardless of power state.

Standard: ANSI A156.5
Fail-Secure avail. Fail-Safe avail.
Header-Mounted
Electromagnetic Lock
Surface-mounted electromagnet at the door header holds an armature plate on the door face. No interaction with the latch or lockset — holding force only (600–1,200 lb typical). Any lockset function can be used; passage trim is most common.

IBC 1010.1.9.8 requires: Motion sensor above door (passive infrared) that releases on approach, OR a pushbutton with ≤15-second delay + signage. Fire alarm must trigger release.

Standard: ANSI A156.23
Always Fail-Safe
Door-Mounted
Electrified Lockset
Solenoid or motor within the lockset chassis retracts the latchbolt upon credential presentation. No frame modification required. Available primarily on mortise platform; electrified storeroom (F04) is most common spec.

Request-to-Exit (RTE/REX) sensor is typically built into the inside lever — occupant presence retracts latch automatically for egress without pressing a button.

Cleaner installation and higher security than electric strike, at higher cost.
Fail-Secure avail. Fail-Safe avail.
Exit Device
Electric Latch Retraction
Motor retracts the exit device latch/bolt upon credential presentation. Used with panic/exit devices only — not a lockset. Specify separately in the hardware schedule as a feature of the exit device.

Common at building main entries where a card reader on the outside triggers latch retraction; occupants exit freely via panic bar. Coordinate with the access control system integrator.

Manufacturers: Von Duprin, Sargent, Corbin, Precision
Fail-Secure avail. Fail-Safe avail.
Fail-Secure · Fail-Locked
Power Loss = Locked
When power is interrupted, the device remains locked. Entry requires a mechanical key or restoration of power.

Use for: Interior security doors, server/IDF rooms, drug storage, storerooms, any opening where unauthorized access during a power failure is the primary risk.

Note: Inside egress must never be impeded — fail-secure applies to the entry control only. Inside lever or panic bar must always allow free egress.
Fail-Safe · Fail-Open
Power Loss = Unlocked
When power is interrupted, the device releases and unlocks. The door operates as an uncontrolled opening until power is restored.

Use for: Egress paths required by life safety code, exterior entries on egress routes, any opening the AHJ requires to fail open. Maglocks are inherently fail-safe and cannot be configured otherwise.

Fire alarm: NFPA 101 requires electrified openings on egress paths to release upon fire alarm signal, regardless of fail mode setting.

Function codes and behaviors vary by manufacturer, hardware series, and platform (mortise vs. cylindrical). ANSI/BHMA A156.13 F-codes apply specifically to mortise locks; cylindrical (A156.2) functions use similar names but different designation systems. For exit device trim functions reference A156.3. Always verify function behavior against manufacturer product data and cut sheets before specifying. Consult a hardware consultant or distributor on projects with complex keying or access control requirements.
