Commercial Roofing
Membrane Systems

STANDARDS: ASTM / NRCA
RATINGS: FM GLOBAL / UL
MIN SLOPE: ¼″:12″
CSI: 07 50 00

Commercial roofing membranes are the primary waterproofing layer in low-slope roofing assemblies. Selection is driven by climate, deck type, chemical exposure, and owner lifecycle expectations. Assemblies are rated as complete systems — confirm FM or UL approval for the specific membrane, insulation, and attachment combination before specifying.

BUR
ASTM D2178 Multi-Ply
Built-Up Roofing
3–5 Plies Hot Asphalt or Cold-Applied Redundant Layers
Composition: Multiple alternating plies of reinforcing felt (fiberglass or organic mat per ASTM D2178) and bitumen — hot asphalt (Type III or IV), cold adhesive, or coal-tar pitch — topped with aggregate ballast or mineral cap sheet.
Seams: Continuous mopped plies with offset laps — no discrete seams. Redundant waterproofing layers.
Ballast: Gravel or aggregate (¾″ river stone typical) adds UV protection and mechanical protection; adds approximately 10–20 psf dead load.
Service life: 15–30 years; well-maintained assemblies have exceeded 40 years.

Attachment Methods

Mechanically Fastened
Method: Membrane is fastened with screws and plates through insulation into the structural deck at lap edges. Membrane field is unfastened.

Wind uplift: Fastener pattern and spacing are engineered to ASCE 7 requirements — corner and perimeter zones require closer fastener spacing than the field.

Most common for new construction on metal deck. Faster installation than fully adhered; lower material cost.
Most Common Metal Deck Faster Install
Fully Adhered
Method: Membrane bonded across its full surface to the insulation or substrate using bonding adhesive — no fasteners penetrate the membrane field.

Advantage: Eliminates fastener pull-through risk and thermal bridging at lap fasteners. Required for some FM wind uplift classifications and for use over lightweight concrete decks.

Higher material and labor cost. Substrate must be clean, dry, flat, and compatible with the adhesive.
No Fasteners Higher Wind Rating Higher Cost
Ballasted
Method: Membrane is loose-laid and held in place by river stone, concrete pavers, or other aggregate ballast — typically 10–12 psf minimum for wind resistance.

Deck requirement: Structural deck must support the ballast dead load — confirm with structural engineer. Most common on cast-in-place concrete decks.

Fastest installation; membrane is not penetrated and can be inspected or replaced without adhesive removal.
Concrete Deck Loose-Laid Easy Future Access
Induction Welded
Method: Manufacturer-specific plates are placed below the membrane; an electromagnetic induction tool activates the plates to weld them to the membrane from above — no penetration through the membrane field.

Use: TPO and PVC only. Combines the wind uplift reliability of mechanical fastening with a continuous unpenetrated membrane field.

Requires manufacturer-certified plates and trained applicator. Higher equipment and labor cost than standard mechanical fastening.
TPO / PVC Only No Field Penetrations Specialty

For reference only. Roofing assemblies are approved as complete systems — membrane, insulation, fasteners, and deck — under FM Global or UL listing. Wind uplift requirements vary by location, building height, and exposure category per ASCE 7. Minimum slope, drainage design, and flashing details are project-specific. Verify all assembly approvals and warranty requirements with the membrane manufacturer and a licensed roofing consultant before specifying.
